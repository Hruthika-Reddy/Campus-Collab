📊 Managerial Economics

Managerial economics applies economic theory and quantitative methods to business decision-making. It bridges economic theory and practice in business management.

1. Demand & Supply Analysis

The fundamental concept that examines the relationship between price and quantity in markets.

Key Concepts:

  • Demand Function: Qd = a - bP (where Qd is quantity demanded, P is price)
  • Supply Function: Qs = c + dP (where Qs is quantity supplied)
  • Market Equilibrium: Where Qd = Qs
Price Elasticity of Demand (Ed) = (%ΔQd) / (%ΔP)

Practical Example: Smartphone Pricing

A company reduces smartphone prices by 10%, leading to a 15% increase in sales:

Ed = 15% / 10% = 1.5 (Elastic Demand)

Interpretation: The 1.5 elasticity means demand is responsive to price changes. A 1% price decrease leads to a 1.5% increase in quantity demanded.

Factors Affecting Elasticity:

Factor Impact on Elasticity
Availability of substitutes More substitutes → More elastic
Necessity vs luxury Luxuries → More elastic
Time horizon Longer time → More elastic

2. Cost & Production Theory

Understanding how production costs behave at different output levels is crucial for pricing and output decisions.

Cost Types:

Cost Type Description Example
Fixed Costs (FC) Don't vary with output Rent, salaries
Variable Costs (VC) Vary with output Raw materials
Marginal Cost (MC) Cost of one more unit MC = ΔTC/ΔQ

Break-even Analysis Example

A bakery has:

  • Fixed costs: $10,000/month
  • Variable cost per cake: $5
  • Selling price per cake: $15
Break-even Quantity = FC / (P - VC) = 10,000 / (15 - 5) = 1,000 cakes/month

Interpretation: The bakery must sell 1,000 cakes each month to cover all costs.

3. Market Structures

Different market environments require different managerial strategies.

Market Type Characteristics Pricing Power Examples
Perfect Competition Many firms, identical products None (price taker) Agricultural commodities
Monopolistic Competition Many firms, differentiated products Some Restaurants, clothing
Oligopoly Few dominant firms Significant Automobiles, airlines
Monopoly Single seller Substantial Utilities, patents

Pricing Strategy in Oligopoly: Game Theory

The prisoner's dilemma applied to airline pricing:

Airline B Airline A
High Price Low Price
High Price A: $10m, B: $10m A: $15m, B: $2m
Low Price A: $2m, B: $15m A: $5m, B: $5m

Nash Equilibrium: Both airlines choose low prices despite better collective outcome with high prices.

📈 Financial Accounting

The systematic recording, reporting, and analysis of financial transactions of a business.

1. Accounting Principles

The foundation of all financial reporting and analysis.

GAAP Principles:

Principle Description
Revenue Recognition Record revenue when earned
Matching Match expenses to related revenues
Consistency Use same methods period to period
Materiality Only record significant items

Accounting Equation:

Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity

Transaction Analysis Example

Transaction: Company purchases $5,000 equipment with $2,000 cash and $3,000 loan.

Account Change
Equipment (Asset) +$5,000
Cash (Asset) -$2,000
Loans Payable (Liability) +$3,000

Equation remains balanced: +$5,000 = +$3,000 + $2,000 (from reduced cash)

2. Financial Statements

The three core reports that communicate financial information.

1. Balance Sheet

Snapshot of financial position at a point in time.

Assets Amount Liabilities & Equity Amount
Cash $50,000 Accounts Payable $20,000
Inventory $30,000 Long-term Debt $100,000
Equipment $120,000 Owner's Equity $80,000
Total Assets $200,000 Total L + E $200,000

2. Income Statement

Financial performance over a period.

Revenue $500,000
Cost of Goods Sold ($300,000)
Gross Profit $200,000
Operating Expenses ($100,000)
Net Income $100,000

3. Cash Flow Statement

Shows cash inflows and outflows from operations, investing, and financing.

3. Ratio Analysis

Key metrics to assess financial health and performance.

Ratio Category Key Ratios Formula Ideal Range
Liquidity Current Ratio Current Assets / Current Liabilities 1.5-3.0
Profitability Net Profit Margin Net Income / Revenue Industry dependent
Leverage Debt-to-Equity Total Debt / Total Equity < 2.0
Efficiency Inventory Turnover COGS / Average Inventory Higher is better

Ratio Analysis Example

Company XYZ has:

  • Current Assets: $150,000
  • Current Liabilities: $75,000
  • Net Income: $50,000
  • Revenue: $500,000
Current Ratio = $150,000 / $75,000 = 2.0 (Healthy liquidity position)
Net Profit Margin = $50,000 / $500,000 = 10% (For comparison with industry average)

📚 Case Studies & Applications

Case Study 1: Pricing Strategy at Apple

Situation: Apple's premium pricing strategy for iPhones

Analysis:

  • Price elasticity analysis shows inelastic demand among loyal customers
  • Skimming pricing strategy for new models
  • Psychological pricing at $999 instead of $1000

Result: Maintained high profit margins despite competition

Case Study 2: Cost Accounting at Toyota

Situation: Implementing lean manufacturing principles

Analysis:

  • Activity-based costing to identify waste
  • Just-in-time inventory management
  • Kaizen continuous improvement

Result: Reduced production costs by 15% over 3 years
